Footwork Improvement Plan (Based on PechPong Footwork Video)
============================================================

Original link: https://youtu.be/lJKOdQ05qUw?is=LCJLmkrahlKzc-3t

Brief overview
--------------

This plan turns the video’s framework into a structured progression you can use in training. It focuses on getting you into neutral quickly after serve/receive, then systematically training the specific movement patterns for different ball placements and finally locking them in with realistic drills.

Phase 1 – Foundations: Preparation and neutral position
-------------------------------------------------------

**Goal:** Make getting back to a strong neutral stance after serve/short receive automatic.

- **Key ideas from transcript:**
  - After serving or pushing short, move into neutral position as fast as possible; don’t stay close or twisted at the table.
  - Avoid lazy one-foot recoveries — use both feet and land with space from the table.
  - From receive, either take one larger jump back or two smaller jumps to regain distance; two small jumps allow better micro-adjustments.
- **Technical focus:**
  - Neutral stance: feet a bit wider than shoulders, weight on the balls of the feet, slight bend in knees, torso relaxed but ready.
  - Symmetric, balanced jump back using both feet, not stepping back with only one leg.

**Drills (2–3 sessions):**

1. **Shadow serve → neutral (no ball)**
   - Perform 10–15 forehand pendulum serves in a row.
   - Immediately after each serve, use both feet to “jump” into neutral with some distance from the table.
   - Check: you are not crossing feet, not over-rotating, and you land balanced and ready to move either way.
2. **Shadow short receive → neutral**
   - Simulate moving in to push short, then perform:
     - Option A: one big jump back.
     - Option B: two smaller jumps back.
   - Alternate A/B and notice which lets you adjust more naturally.
3. **Video check**
   - Record 1–2 minutes of these shadow movements.
   - Confirm you are:
     - Moving immediately after contact.
     - Landing with enough space to swing.
     - Not drifting sideways unintentionally.

Phase 2 – Deep ball to the middle (physically hardest pattern)
--------------------------------------------------------------

**Goal:** Handle a deep push to the middle with a strong forehand without getting jammed.

- **Key ideas from transcript:**
  - Deep to the middle is one of the hardest balls physically if you don’t give yourself enough space.
  - Movement pattern: move a bit away from the table and to your left (for a right-hander), right foot initiates, left foot creates room.
  - Right foot moves slightly back, left foot steps slightly in, creating space to rotate and swing.
  - Immediately recover to a position that matches your placement (wide forehand vs backhand).

**Technical focus:**

- First move is with the right foot, but both feet are active—think “push and glide” rather than stepping one foot at a time.
- Create lateral and backward space so contact is in front of your body, not jammed near your hip.
- After the stroke, recover depth and angle based on where you played the ball.

**Drills (2–4 sessions):**

1. **Shadow movement: deep middle**
   - From neutral, simulate a deep middle push.
   - Perform the pattern: right foot slightly back, left foot slightly in, rotate and swing, then recover.
   - Repeat slowly 10–15 times, then at game tempo.
2. **Partner feed: serve + deep middle**
   - You serve short; partner always pushes deep to your middle.
   - Your job: apply the movement pattern and make a strong forehand.
   - Focus on:
     - Getting back far enough before swinging.
     - Contacting in front of your body.
3. **Robot/multiball: fixed deep middle**
   - Set robot or coach feed to one deep-middle location.
   - Start in ready position with space from the table.
   - Each ball: move, loop, recover fully to neutral before the next ball.

Phase 3 – Medium-long and high balls to the middle
--------------------------------------------------

**Goal:** Confidently attack medium-long/low and medium-long/high balls over the table in the middle.

- **Key ideas from transcript:**
  - Medium-long, low pushes near the end line require patience—wait until the ball comes off the table.
  - Movement is still right-foot-first into the ball, then a quick recovery back out.
  - Medium-long, high balls can be attacked earlier over the table but require a two-part movement: a small jump then a powerful step in.

**Technical focus:**

- For low medium-long balls:
  - Time the step so you contact after the bounce clears the table edge.
  - Move into the table with right-then-left, then quickly jump back after the stroke.
- For high medium-long balls:
  - Combine a small two-foot jump with a strong step in with the left foot (for right-handers).
  - Start the backswing as you step in so you can attack decisively.

**Drills (2–4 sessions):**

1. **Partner feed: medium-long low middle**
   - Partner pushes consistently medium-long and low to your middle.
   - You move in with right-then-left, loop, and then recover quickly backwards.
2. **Partner feed: medium-long high middle**
   - Partner sends slightly higher, medium-long balls over the table to your middle.
   - Practice the two-part movement: small jump, then big step in with the left, aggressive stroke, then recovery.
3. **Mixed feed: low vs high**
   - Partner alternates low and high medium-long balls to the middle.
   - You must read height and choose the correct movement pattern in real time.

Phase 4 – Wide forehand and wide backhand patterns
--------------------------------------------------

**Goal:** Build reliable patterns for common wide pushes to forehand and backhand.

- **Key ideas from transcript:**
  - Normal wide push to the forehand is a good starting movement for beginners—one simple pattern.
  - For the wide forehand, left foot often initiates, then right follows, allowing you to attack strongly.
  - For wide backhand, you can use either a one-step movement (big step with left foot) or a two-foot movement, depending on preference and style.

**Technical focus:**

- Wide forehand:
  - Initiate with the left foot, then bring the right foot with a small jump.
  - Maintain balance so you can recover center or even slightly to the backhand side depending on placement.
- Wide backhand:
  - Try the one-step approach (big step with left foot) and the two-foot approach (push off right, land both in a stable stance).
  - Choose the one that feels more stable and repeatable under pressure.

**Drills (3–5 sessions):**

1. **Shadow wide forehand**
   - From neutral, simulate a push to wide forehand.
   - Move left-then-right, swing, then recover to neutral or slightly toward your backhand position.
2. **Partner feed: fixed wide forehand**
   - Partner gives a consistent wide forehand ball after a short serve/push pattern.
   - Focus on getting there with one smooth movement and recovering right after the shot.
3. **Partner feed: fixed wide backhand (two patterns)**
   - First block: use one-step left-foot movement only.
   - Second block: use two-foot movement (push off right, land both).
   - Notice which gives you better balance and power.
4. **Alternating wide forehand / wide backhand**
   - Partner alternates pushes between wide forehand and wide backhand.
   - You must switch between the two movement patterns without losing stance quality.

Phase 5 – Integration with realistic drills
-------------------------------------------

**Goal:** Combine all movement patterns into realistic point-like sequences.

- **Key ideas from transcript:**
  - The coach emphasizes drills like multiball, partner simulations, and robot work to integrate movements.
  - You should not “cheat” (pre-move before the ball is fed) or treat it as casual cardio; it must match game intensity and seriousness.

**Integration drills (ongoing):**

1. **Serve + fixed follow-up**
   - Pick one scenario (e.g., deep middle, wide forehand, medium-long high middle).
   - Serve short, partner always plays that scenario ball.
   - Focus exclusively on perfect movement and recovery pattern for that situation.
2. **Serve + random follow-up from a small menu**
   - Choose 2–3 scenarios (e.g., deep middle, wide forehand, wide backhand).
   - Partner randomly picks one after your serve; you must read and execute the correct movement.
3. **Multiball/robot in-out pattern**
   - One short ball to bring you in, then a predetermined follow-up (deep middle, medium-long middle, wide forehand, etc.).
   - Emphasize in-and-out movement: into the table, then back out to neutral.

Phase 6 – Practice structure and progression
--------------------------------------------

**Suggested 4-week structure (assuming 3 sessions/week):**

- **Week 1:**
  - Warm-up: Phase 1 prep movements every session.
  - Focus: Phase 2 (deep middle) and Phase 3 (medium-long low).
- **Week 2:**
  - Warm-up: Phase 1 + deep middle shadow work.
  - Focus: Finish Phase 3 (medium-long high) and start Phase 4 (wide forehand).
- **Week 3:**
  - Warm-up: Mixed shadow of all patterns learned so far.
  - Focus: Phase 4 (wide backhand) and Phase 5 integration drills (serve + fixed follow-ups).
- **Week 4:**
  - Warm-up: Quick review of each movement pattern.
  - Focus: Phase 5 full integration (serve + random follow-ups, multiball/robot in-out drills).

Review and adjustment
---------------------

- Record short clips of yourself doing:
  - Serve → neutral.
  - Deep middle pattern.
  - Medium-long low/high middle patterns.
  - Wide forehand and wide backhand patterns.
- Compare your movements to the cues in the video:
  - Are you giving yourself enough space away from the table?
  - Is contact in front of the body, not jammed near the hip?
  - Are your recoveries fast enough to handle the next ball?
- Every 2–3 weeks, pick the weakest pattern and make it the primary focus of your drills until it feels natural.
